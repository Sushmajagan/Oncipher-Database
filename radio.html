Radiotherapy

Radiotherapy, or radiation therapy, is a crucial component in the treatment of breast cancer, primarily aimed at destroying remaining cancer cells after surgery and reducing the risk of recurrence. It utilizes high-energy rays, such as X-rays or protons, to target and kill cancer cells while sparing surrounding healthy tissue.

Radiotherapy is typically recommended in the following scenarios:

1.Post-Surgery Treatment:

  • After breast-conserving surgery (lumpectomy), radiotherapy is standard to eliminate residual cancer cells and significantly reduce the risk of local recurrence by 50-60% 14. Following a mastectomy, especially if there are positive lymph nodes or close surgical margins, radiotherapy can improve overall survival rates.

    2.Neoadjuvant Therapy:

    In some cases, radiotherapy may be administered after neoadjuvant systemic therapy (NST) to further decrease recurrence risk.

    3.Palliative Care:

    For advanced breast cancer that has metastasized to other areas of the body, radiotherapy can help alleviate symptoms and control disease progression.

    Types of Radiotherapy

    1.External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT):

    The most common form, where radiation is delivered from a machine to the entire breast or specific areas.

    Whole Breast Irradiation (WBI):

    Typically administered over 5-6 weeks, with recent studies supporting shorter courses (about 3 weeks) that are equally effective.

    Partial Breast Irradiation (PBI):

    Targets only the tumor bed and surrounding tissues, which may be suitable for select early-stage patients.

    2.Brachytherapy:

    Involves placing radioactive sources directly into or near the tumor site. This method can deliver higher doses of radiation to a localized area while minimizing exposure to surrounding healthy tissue.

    3.Hypofractionated Radiation Therapy:

    Delivers larger doses of radiation over fewer sessions (typically 3-4 weeks), showing similar effectiveness to conventional schedules while potentially reducing side effects 45.

    Planning and Administration

    Before starting radiotherapy, a detailed planning process is conducted:

    Treatment Planning:

    Involves imaging studies to determine the precise area needing treatment and calculating the appropriate radiation dose.
