Hormone Therapy

Hormone therapy, also known as endocrine therapy, is a critical treatment option for certain types of breast cancer that are hormone receptor-positive (HR+). This therapy works by targeting the hormones estrogen and progesterone, which can fuel the growth of some breast cancer cells. Hormone therapy functions primarily in two ways:

  • Blocking Hormones: Some drugs prevent estrogen and progesterone from binding to their receptors on cancer cells, effectively blocking their growth signals.
  • Lowering Hormone Levels: Other treatments reduce the overall levels of these hormones in the body, thereby limiting the cancer cells' access to them.

Types of Hormone Therapy

 1.Tamoxifen: An anti-estrogen medication that blocks estrogen receptors in breast tissue. It is commonly used for both premenopausal and postmenopausal women.

2.Aromatase Inhibitors (AIs):

These drugs lower estrogen levels by inhibiting the aromatase enzyme responsible for converting androgens into estrogens. AIs are typically used in postmenopausal women and include:
  • Anastrozole (Arimidex)
  • Letrozole (Femara)
  • Exemestane (Aromasin)

    • Ovarian Suppression:

    In premenopausal women, treatments may involve surgical removal of the ovaries (oophorectomy) or medications that suppress ovarian function, often combined with tamoxifen or AIs.

    Less Common Therapies:

    Other treatments may include selective estrogen receptor modulators (SERMs) or newer agents like CDK4/6 inhibitors (e.g., palbociclib) that are sometimes combined with hormone therapies to enhance effectiveness. Source
