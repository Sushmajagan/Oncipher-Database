A cervical biopsy is a crucial diagnostic procedure used to obtain tissue samples from the cervix for examination under a microscope. It is primarily performed when cervical screening tests, such as Pap smears or HPV tests, yield abnormal results. The biopsy helps determine the presence of precancerous changes or cervical cancer.

Types of Cervical Biopsies

1.Colposcopy-Guided Biopsy:

Procedure:

 After a colposcopic examination, where a colposcope provides a magnified view of the cervix, the physician identifies abnormal areas. A small tissue sample is then taken using biopsy forceps.

Advantages:

 This method allows for targeted sampling of suspicious lesions.

Colposcopy

2.Punch Biopsy:

Procedure:

A specialized instrument called punch biopsy forceps is used to remove small samples of cervical tissue from the surface of the cervix.

Indications:

 Typically performed during a colposcopy when abnormalities are detected.

Punch Biopsy

3.Loop Electrosurgical Excision Procedure (LEEP):

Procedure:

 A thin wire loop heated by an electrical current is used to excise abnormal cervical tissue along with a margin of healthy tissue. This method also cauterizes blood vessels to minimize bleeding.

Indications:

 Used for both diagnosis and treatment of precancerous lesions.

Loop Electrosurgical Excision Procedure

4.Cone Biopsy (Conization):

Procedure:

 A cone-shaped section of cervical tissue is surgically removed, which may include both the endocervical and exocervical tissues. This can be done using a scalpel, laser, or LEEP technique.

Indications:

Often utilized when there is a need for deeper tissue sampling to assess invasive cancer.

5.Endocervical Curettage (ECC):

Procedure:

 A curette (a small, spoon-shaped instrument) is used to scrape cells from the endocervical canal.

Indications:

Performed when there is suspicion of abnormalities in the canal that may not be visible during colposcopy.

Procedure Steps

Preparation:

1.Patients are advised to avoid vaginal intercourse, douching, and using tampons for 24 hours before the procedure.

2.The procedure is usually scheduled when the patient is not menstruating.

During the Procedure:

1.The patient lies on an examination table in a lithotomy position.

2.A speculum is inserted into the vagina to hold it open.

3.The cervix may be washed with a vinegar solution to enhance visibility of abnormal cells.

4.Depending on the type of biopsy, either forceps or a heated wire loop is used to collect tissue samples.

Post-Procedure Care:

1.Patients may experience light bleeding or discharge following the biopsy.

2.Instructions typically include avoiding tampons and sexual intercourse for a specified period to allow healing.

Risks and Complications

 While cervical biopsies are generally safe, potential risks include:
  • Mild cramping or discomfort during and after the procedure.
  • Light bleeding or spotting post-biopsy.
  • Rarely, infection or excessive bleeding may occur.

    • Results Interpretation

     Tissue samples obtained from biopsies are sent to a pathology laboratory for microscopic examination.

    Results can indicate:
    • Normal cervical tissue.
    • Precancerous changes (CIN).
    • Invasive cervical cancer.
