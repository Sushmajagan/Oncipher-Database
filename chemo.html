Chemotherapy

Chemotherapy is a vital treatment option for breast cancer, utilizing cytotoxic drugs to destroy cancer cells. It can be administered in various settings, depending on the cancer's stage and characteristics.

Purposes of Chemotherapy

  • Adjuvant Therapy: Given after surgery to reduce the risk of cancer recurrence, especially if there are indications that cancer cells may have spread.
  • Neoadjuvant Therapy: Administered before surgery to shrink tumors, making them easier to remove. This is particularly useful for larger or aggressive tumors.
  • Palliative Treatment: For advanced or metastatic breast cancer, chemotherapy helps control symptoms and manage the disease's progression.

    Common Chemotherapy Drugs

    Chemotherapy regimens often involve combinations of drugs to enhance effectiveness. Commonly used classes and specific drugs include:

           Anthracyclines:
    • Doxorubicin (Adriamycin)
    • Epirubicin (Ellence)

      • Taxanes:
    • Paclitaxel (Taxol)
    • Docetaxel (Taxotere)

      • Platinum Agents:
    • Carboplatin
    • Cisplatin

      • Other Agents:
    • Cyclophosphamide
    • Capecitabine
    • Eribulin

      • Regimens may combine these drugs, such as AC (doxorubicin and cyclophosphamide) or TC (docetaxel and cyclophosphamide).

      Administration of Chemotherapy

      Chemotherapy can be administered via:          
      • Intravenous (IV) Infusion: Most commonly through a central line or port, allowing for easier access during multiple treatment sessions.
      • Oral Medications: Some chemotherapy drugs are available in pill form, providing an alternative to IV administration. The schedule and duration of chemotherapy depend on the specific regimen prescribed, typically involving cycles of treatment followed by rest periods to allow the body to recover.
