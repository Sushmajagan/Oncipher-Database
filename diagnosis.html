Clinical Examination

Breast Examination: A healthcare professional assesses the breasts for any lumps or abnormalities.

Imaging Tests

1.Mammography:
 A low-dose X-ray specifically for breast tissue.

*Advantages: Cost-effective and widely used for screening.
*Limitations: Less effective in women with dense breasts, leading to higher false-positive rates in younger women.	Source

2.Ultrasound:
 Uses sound waves to create images of breast tissues.

*Advantages: Real-time imaging and cost-effective.
*Limitations: Cannot detect microcalcifications, which may indicate early cancer.	Source

3.Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI):
 Utilizes a magnetic field and radio waves for detailed breast images.

*Advantages: Particularly beneficial for evaluating tumors not visible on mammograms or in dense breast tissue.
*Limitations: Can overestimate tumor size due to surrounding abnormal tissue.	Source

4.Positron Emission Tomography-Computed Tomography (PET-CT):
 Combines metabolic imaging with anatomical imaging.

*Advantages: Non-invasive and provides detailed images.
*Limitations: High cost and less effective for small tumors.	Source

5.Breast Specific Gamma Imaging (BSGI):
 A nuclear medicine test that uses a radiotracer to visualize breast tissues.

*Advantages: Effective for detecting small lesions, unaffected by breast density.
*Limitations: High radiation dose, not suitable for routine screening.	Source

Biopsy Procedures
	The definitive diagnosis of breast cancer is made through biopsy, which involves removing tissue samples for laboratory analysis.

	1.Fine Needle Aspiration Cytology (FNAC):
			A thin needle is used to withdraw cells from a suspicious area for quick analysis.

	2.Core Needle Biopsy (CNB):
		A larger needle extracts a core of tissue, providing more comprehensive samples than FNAC.

	3.Sentinel Lymph Node Biopsy (SLNB):
		This procedure determines if cancer has spread to lymph nodes, guiding treatment decisions
