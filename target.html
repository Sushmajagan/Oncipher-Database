Target Therapy

Targeted therapy is a specialized form of treatment that focuses on specific characteristics of cancer cells, aiming to inhibit their growth and spread while minimizing damage to normal cells. This approach is particularly effective for certain types of breast cancer, especially those that are hormone receptor-positive or HER2-positive.

Mechanism

 Targeted therapies work by interfering with specific molecules involved in cancer cell growth and survival. Unlike traditional chemotherapy, which affects all rapidly dividing cells, targeted therapies are designed to focus on particular pathways or proteins that are crucial for the cancer's proliferation.

Types of Targeted Therapy

HER2-Targeted Therapies

Trastuzumab (Herceptin):

A monoclonal antibody that binds to the HER2 protein on the surface of cancer cells, blocking signals that promote cell division. It is commonly used for HER2-positive breast cancers.

Pertuzumab (Perjeta):

Often used in combination with trastuzumab and chemotherapy, it also targets HER2 but works through a different mechanism, enhancing the effectiveness of trastuzumab. Trastuzumab Emtansine (Kadcyla):

A conjugate of trastuzumab and a chemotherapy drug (emtansine) that delivers cytotoxic agents directly to HER2-positive cells.

Hormone Receptor-Positive Therapies

CDK4/6 Inhibitors:

Such as palbociclib (Ibrance), ribociclib (Kisqali), and abemaciclib (Verzenios). These drugs inhibit proteins that promote cell cycle progression, often used in combination with hormone therapy for ER-positive breast cancer.

PIK3CA Inhibitors:

Alpelisib (Piqray) targets cancers with specific mutations in the PIK3CA gene, often used alongside hormone therapy like fulvestrant.

PARP Inhibitors

Olaparib (Lynparza) and Talazoparib (Talzenna):

These drugs are particularly effective for patients with BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations, targeting DNA repair mechanisms in cancer cells.

Immunotherapy

Pembrolizumab (Keytruda) and Atezolizumab (Tecentriq):

These are checkpoint inhibitors that enhance the immune system's ability to recognize and destroy cancer cells. They are typically used in triple-negative breast cancer or in combination with other treatments. Source
